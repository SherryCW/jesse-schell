## Route by project stage

### Concept

Prioritize essential experience, context, theme, four elements, target player, and largest-risk prototype. Avoid detailed economy and content production unless they are existential assumptions.

### Core-loop prototype

Prioritize state–action–feedback, intrinsic value, challenge/skill, meaningful choice, clarity, and observed behavior. Test the loop without progression rewards first.

### Vertical slice

Prioritize unity across four elements, interface, interest curve, technical maturity, production repeatability, and pitch clarity. Separate “beautiful once” from “repeatably producible.”

### Production

Prioritize content pipeline risks, documentation, team alignment, accessibility, pacing, balance, and regression playtests. Protect the essential experience from scope-driven dilution.

### Soft launch

Prioritize telemetry validity, onboarding vs third-session behavior, economy loops, dominant strategies, segment differences, payment trust, and guardrail metrics.

### Live operations

Prioritize thirtieth-session value, community identity, governance, economy inflation, content cadence, fairness perception, churn causes, and cumulative harm.

### Redesign

Prioritize evidence-backed problem definition, preserved essential experience, migration cost, player identity loss, and reversible experiments. Do not erase loved behavior merely because it is architecturally inconvenient.

## Lens portfolio rule

Build a portfolio with one lens from each required layer:

1. **Experience/player**: emotion, essential experience, context, player, needs, motivation.
2. **System/expression**: four elements, state machine, balance, interface, curve, story, world, character, community.
3. **Evidence/risk**: problem statement, risk elimination, playtest, technology maturity, responsibility.

Add one **commercial/production** lens only when it can kill feasibility. Add one **social/responsibility** lens whenever identity, payment, ranking, user content, minors, privacy, health, or harassment is in scope.

## Conflict examples

- A more immersive VR interaction loses to comfort and accessibility when the same essential experience has a safer embodiment.
- A high-converting reward loop loses to trust and responsibility when it exploits confusion, minors, or irreversible spending.
- A designer's theme interpretation loses to repeated player behavior only after checking whether the test context and target audience are valid.
- A client request is treated as a hypothesis about their objective; preserve the objective while testing alternative solutions.
